By Ayuka Kawakami
July 24, 2022

Heat-related illnesses such as heat exhaustion and deadly heatstroke happen when our body cannot cool down enough. In NYC, the most heat-related death occurs in an un-air-conditioned home, either because they do not own one or do not use one even during the heatwaves. As NYC's living and utility costs are among the highest in the nation, owning and using air conditioning is too expensive for many living with limited financial resources. While more than 90% of NYC households have air conditioning, access is lower in the low-income neighborhoods. Consequently, those communities suffer heat-related illnesses the most.

Climate change doesn’t affect everyone equally

NYC Health Department reported that Black New Yorkers are subjected to higher poverty rates and lower access to air conditioning and neighborhood cooling resources. As a result, they are more likely to die from heat stress, with death rates two times higher than among other New Yorkers. This is the result of past and current social inequality that provided the communities less economic opportunities and healthy housing options. While more than 25% of those who died during the heatwave had an electric fan on, only using an electric fan without an AC cannot always prevent death during extreme heat.

Air conditioning is no longer a luxury but a life-saving equipment

NYC is getting hotter, and heatwaves are getting longer. By 2080, it is projected that NYC will have more than 60 days with temperatures over 90F degrees. “As we experience a changing climate, air conditioning is no longer a luxury but a life-saving equipment. Home cooling in the summer should be treated like heat in the winter: necessary to survival, particularly for those who are most at risk,” NYC Health Department explains. While NYC provides cooling centers and free air conditioners for those who qualify, we need to do more to protect the low-income communities from the increasingly hotter New York.

While we need to be aware of the environmental impacts of air conditioning, it is the best defense against heat-related illnesses. NYC mayor’s office suggests interventions such as regulations requiring landlords to provide working air conditioning to tenants and prohibiting electricity disconnections during hot weather even if bills are unpaid. While these regulations may be necessary, they may not be sustainable solutions in the long run. Instead, we need to use the air conditioning responsibly among more households while limiting unnecessarily excessive usage of commercial establishments. In the hotter future, distributing energy-efficient air conditioning to all at risk, combined with cleaner energy sources, may be necessary to protect the lives of all New Yorkers.
